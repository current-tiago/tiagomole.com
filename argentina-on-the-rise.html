Argentina on the Rise

Austrian economics in the 21st century — the Milei experiment

March 2025

Javier Milei took office on the 10th of December, 2023, with one clear mandate: save the Argentinian economy. The monthly inflation rate the month before was 160.9%, the CPI increase was 12.8%, and the Central Bank had raised interest rates to 133% — its sixth hike in a single year.

Milei, a complete outsider in Argentine politics until 2021 when he won election to Congress, led a campaign described by many as a revolution against the political order — specifically against the Peronista political brand that had dominated Argentinian politics for the past century. To understand why he won, and what he inherited, you must understand a century of extraordinary economic failure.

The Lost Century

Milei promised Argentinians an end to what many called the "lost century" following the First World War. The claim has historical basis: Argentina was the sixth largest economy in the world per capita before the outbreak of that war, sitting behind only England, France and the United States. Its export-driven agricultural economy had produced extraordinary wealth over the course of the 19th century.

The conventional explanation for the subsequent collapse points to the Great Depression and the trade restrictions of the 1930s. But the numbers complicate this story. By 1940, the Argentinian economy was still broadly keeping pace with nations such as France, Italy and Germany — all of which were deeply protectionist in their trade policy at the time, whether by ideological design (autarky in Germany's case) or practical necessity. If shared protectionism were the sole explanation, Argentina's deviation from the pack would have been visible far earlier. Something else was driving the decline.

Extractive Institutions

In Daron Acemoglu and James Robinson's Why Nations Fail, the authors argue that nations with politically extractive institutions — those that seek to preserve the elite structure rather than open themselves to genuine pluralism — tend towards economic stagnation. The political establishment creates an economically extractive society, consolidating ownership and regulation in the hands of those who already hold power. Argentina is the book's most instructive case study.

Between 1930 and 1983, Argentina underwent no fewer than six military coups. Under the various military dictatorships — broken intermittently by the leadership of Juan Domingo Perón, himself a product of the 1943 coup — GDP growth dropped dramatically and repeatedly.

Juan Domingo Perón is perhaps the single most important figure in the politics of Argentina. His party, the Justicialist Party — colloquially called the Peronist Party — has ruled Argentina for a total of 40 years. Peronismo advocates for strong government presence in the market, heavy protectionist trade policy and a welfare system so all-encompassing that, in recent years, even Netflix subscriptions were state-subsidised.

Here lies the crux of the problem. For the last 120 years, Argentina operated in a fiscal deficit in 110 of them. Perón and successive Peronist leaders such as Cristina Kirchner sought to buy the votes of the population through reckless state expenditure, expansive protectionism that left Argentina woefully under-industrialised, and with a currency that would inflate on a whim. The result was a compounding cycle of debt, devaluation and dependence that reduced one of the world's most naturally endowed economies to a state of perpetual crisis.

What Milei Inherited

It can therefore be comprehensively stated that Milei inherited a nation in crisis — not just cyclically, but structurally. The institutions of the state had been engineered over decades to serve the interests of those who controlled them, rather than the population that funded them. The economy was not merely broken; it had been broken in a particular direction, by a particular set of decisions taken over a very long time.

What Milei offered in response was radical: a wholesale rejection of the Keynesian consensus that had underwritten Peronist economics, in favour of the Austrian school — the tradition of von Mises and Hayek, which argues that markets, when left to function freely, allocate resources more effectively than any central planner ever could. His campaign symbol, a chainsaw, was not merely theatrical. It was a statement of intent.

Whether the experiment succeeds — whether Argentina truly rises — depends on questions that go beyond economics: whether institutions can be reformed without being destroyed, whether populations exhausted by crisis can tolerate short-term pain for long-term gain, and whether a country so long defined by political extraction can learn a different way to organise itself. The answer, whichever way it falls, will be one of the most important economic stories of the decade.
