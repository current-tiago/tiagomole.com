McDonald's × Travis Scott

How a quarter pounder and a rapper changed fast food marketing forever

2023

Going into 2020, McDonald's were flying high. Annual sales had increased 5.9% — the highest growth in a decade — and consolidated revenue had reached $21 billion. With a brand value of roughly $48 billion, McDonald's was one of the most recognisable companies in the world. Then COVID arrived, and with it, lockdowns that threatened the entire system: stores forced closed, supply chains disrupted, a global market turned upside down.

What happened next became one of the most instructive case studies in modern brand strategy. McDonald's response to the pandemic years was multifaceted, but at its creative heart was a single collaboration: on September 8th, 2020, McDonald's released the Travis Scott Meal — a medium Sprite, a quarter pounder with bacon, and fries with barbecue sauce — which ran until October 4th. A 26-day partnership that changed the industry.

The Idea

Celebrity partnerships in fast food are nothing new. Sprite's annual Christmas campaign with LeBron James, Pizza Hut's 1998 advertisement with Mikhail Gorbachev — brands have long used famous faces to shift product. But full creative collaborations with artists, where the celebrity had genuine ownership over the aesthetic and strategy of the campaign, were rare.

"Brands historically have told celebrities how to say their message. It's very clear that Travis Scott and his team have gotten through to these brands that they have a very clear aesthetic, messaging and strategy." — Black Robbins, Ludlow Ventures

The Travis Scott partnership was different in its depth of commitment. McDonald's effectively delegated creative authority to Scott and his team, allowing the campaign to feel authentic to his audience rather than manufactured. The menu item itself was deliberately understated — there was no new product, just Scott's favourite existing order repackaged as an event. This was the insight: the product was not the food, but the connection.

The Social Media Explosion

McDonald's spent just $6.8 million on the campaign. By any traditional advertising standard, this is modest for a brand of that scale. The return, however, was anything but modest. The campaign's spread was almost entirely organic, fuelled by fan content on platforms where McDonald's had little natural presence with younger audiences.

On TikTok alone: the hashtag #traviscottburger accumulated approximately 200 million views; #travisscottmeal reached 100 million views; and #travisscottmcdonalds a further 77 million. Based on conservative estimates of revenue per TikTok view, these three hashtags alone represent roughly $400,000 in accumulated platform revenue — and this says nothing of the sales impact at the register.

From a strategy perspective, the campaign falls clearly into the market penetration quadrant of the Ansoff Matrix: no new product, no new market — but a deliberate extension of reach into a younger, more culturally connected demographic (aged 11–24) that had been quietly drifting away from the Golden Arches for years. Porter's framework would classify this as a differentiation strategy, but the differentiation was achieved almost entirely through promotion rather than product innovation. The $6.8 million spend generated disproportionate returns precisely because of the organic amplification Scott's audience provided.

The Numbers

To isolate the campaign's financial impact, the quarterly revenue data from 2019 and 2021 (both years without celebrity meal campaigns) provides a useful baseline. In those years, McDonald's revenues followed a predictable seasonal pattern:

Q1 → Q2 Average
+11.4%
Q2 → Q3 Average
+3.35%
Q3 → Q4 Average
−8.25%

Against this baseline, 2020 — the year of the Travis Scott collaboration — tells a striking story. The Travis Scott campaign ran across Q3 and Q4. In that Q2-to-Q3 window, revenue grew 44%. The baseline for that same period: 3.35%.

The obvious caveat is that 2020 was an outlier year in every direction. Lockdowns being enforced and lifted unpredictably across the world make clean comparison genuinely difficult — the Q1-to-Q2 drop of 20% reflects the initial COVID shock, not any campaign failure. But the recovery slope of that Q3 surge, against the backdrop of an industry in turmoil, is extraordinary by any measure.

What It Proved

McDonald's collaboration with Travis Scott was essential to its performance in the COVID years. It drove revenue, reconnected the brand with a demographic it had been losing, transformed what a promotional meal could mean, and established McDonald's as a creative force in the business world — one prepared to push the boundaries of expected conduct for a restaurant chain.

It also pioneered a template. Following the Scott campaign, McDonald's ran collaborations with J Balvin, BTS, Mariah Carey and others — each time leveraging the artist's existing audience rather than manufacturing one from scratch. The industry followed. The celebrity meal, once a gimmick, became a strategic instrument.

The lesson is not simply about fast food. It is about what happens when a brand trusts an artist enough to step back: authenticity becomes the product, and authenticity — unlike a burger — scales infinitely on social media.
